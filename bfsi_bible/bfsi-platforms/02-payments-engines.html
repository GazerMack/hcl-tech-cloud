VI.2 · Payments engines — Volante, Form3, ACI, Finastra, and the modern payments hub

A payments engine is the piece of software that turns ‘I want to pay £100 to my electrician’ into a sequence of authoritative, regulator-acceptable instructions across the right rails — Faster Payments, SEPA, FedNow, UPI, SWIFT, RTGS — and back into a settled, reconciled, reportable transaction. This topic gives you fluent vocabulary in the major engines (Volante, Form3, ACI, Finastra, FIS, Fiserv, Temenos Payments Hub, Bottomline, Montran), the rails they connect to, and the design choices a techno-functional leader makes when buying, replacing or modernising one.

0. Primer — schemes, rails and engines, said three different ways Basic

Primer — read this even if you think you know it

Most BFSI conversations conflate three different things and call them all ‘payments’. They are very different. A scheme is the rule book and central infrastructure (UPI, SEPA, Faster Payments, FedNow, SWIFT). A rail is the network and the messages used to move money on a scheme. A payments engine (or hub) is the software inside the bank that prepares, validates, signs, sends, receives, settles and reports those messages. Schemes and rails are mostly bought-from; engines are almost always bought (rarely built) from one of 8–10 specialist vendors.

0.1 IT-side anchor — your email is a payments engine in miniature

From what you already know — your laptop

When you click ‘Send’ in your email client, the client doesn’t know how mail will get to the recipient. It hands the message to a Mail Transfer Agent (your email provider), which figures out the recipient’s domain, looks up the right route, formats the message in the right protocol (SMTP), signs it (DKIM), and tracks whether it bounced. That is what a payments engine does for money: a corporate Treasury or a retail customer presses ‘Pay’; the engine decides which rail (SEPA Instant? SWIFT? card?), formats the right ISO 20022 or local message, signs it, sends it, tracks the settlement, and reconciles the response.

0.2 BFSI-side anchor — what happened when your salary landed at 9 a.m.

From what you already know — your bank account

On the first of the month a payment file arrives at your employer’s bank. In India it is a NACH or RTGS batch; in the UK a Bacs file; in the US an ACH (Automated Clearing House) NACHA file; in the EU a SEPA pain.001 message; in cross-border a SWIFT MT103 (or, increasingly, an ISO 20022 pacs.008). The employer’s bank validates, screens for sanctions, decides which rail to use, sends to the scheme, receives confirmation, debits the employer, credits the employee’s bank, and notifies all parties — all in minutes for instant rails, in batch for ACH. The payments engine is the middle of that story and the part the bank is most likely to have bought from a specialist vendor.

0.3 The engine, the hub, and the gateway — three words

  • Payments engine — single-rail, focused: ‘the SWIFT engine’, ‘the SEPA engine’, ‘the ACH engine’. Often a vendor module within a larger suite.
  • Payments hub — multi-rail, single service: customer-initiated payment goes to one place, the hub picks the rail, normalises the data, runs the common controls (sanctions, fraud, AML, limits, duplicate-check), and routes. The architectural target of every modernisation programme since ~2015.
  • Payments gateway — usually means a merchant-side acceptance gateway (Stripe, Razorpay, Adyen, Worldpay) that consumes cards / wallets / Open Banking. Different industry, similar word.

0.4 The seven control points every engine implements

  • Validation — schema, IBAN/BIC checksum, balance check, beneficiary masterdata.
  • Sanctions and watchlist screening — OFAC, EU, UK OFSI, India MEA, UN, internal lists.
  • Fraud / Authorised Push Payment (APP) screening — behavioural risk score, Confirmation of Payee.
  • AML / transaction monitoring — real-time and post-event; case generation.
  • Limits and authorisation — customer limits, bank limits, scheme limits, regulatory limits.
  • Routing and message transformation — pick the rail, build the message in its dialect.
  • Settlement, reconciliation and reporting — match what we sent against what cleared; produce regulatory and customer reports.

1. Why payments engines are the most-modernised BFSI platform Basic

Why is the payments engine the single most-modernised platform in BFSI in 2024–2026?

  1. ISO 20022 forced the issue. Cross-border SWIFT MT retires 22 November 2025; Fedwire migrated 14 March 2025; CHAPS migrated June 2023; SEPA, RTGS-India, FedNow are all ISO 20022 natively. Old engines built around fixed-format MT messages cannot carry the rich structured data without rework.
  2. Instant payments became mandatory. EU Instant Payments Regulation (adopted 2024) made SEPA Instant mandatory: receive by 9 January 2025, send by 9 October 2025, with Verification of Payee. UPI in India, FedNow in the US (live July 2023), Faster Payments in the UK, NPP in Australia, PIX in Brazil, FAST in Singapore — instant is the new normal.
  3. Fraud reimbursement changed the maths. UK PSR mandatory APP fraud reimbursement since October 2024 splits the cost 50/50 between sending and receiving PSPs; engines must score in real time and decide whether to challenge.
  4. Cloud-native is now table stakes. Form3, ACI Enterprise Payments Platform, Volante VolPay and Temenos Payments Hub all run on Kubernetes; older monolith deployments are being retired.
  5. Payments-as-a-Service. Smaller banks and fintechs increasingly outsource the engine entirely (Form3, ClearBank, Banking Circle, Currencycloud).
  6. AI and agentic payments. Generative AI is being applied to investigations, payment-narrative enrichment, sanctions hit-handling, and exception operations.

3.3 Settlement models — the bit the regulator cares about

  • RTGS (Real-Time Gross Settlement) — each payment settled individually in central-bank money, typically high-value. Fedwire, CHAPS, Target2, RTGS-India.
  • Net settlement — payments accumulated and settled in net positions at intervals. CHIPS, ACH, Bacs.
  • Deferred Net Settlement (DNS) for instant — instant rails settle in central-bank money on a net basis at scheduled cycles (FedNow, FPS, SEPA Instant); the customer experiences immediacy, the bank experiences cycles.
  • Pre-funded — participant deposits a balance with the operator; payments debit that balance in real-time. Used by some instant rails (FedNow liquidity model).
  • Bilateral — between two correspondent banks via nostro / vostro accounts. Cross-border SWIFT is the canonical example.

3.4 ISO 20022 — the format that ate the world

ISO 20022 messages used by payment engines (memorise these abbreviations; you will hear them constantly):

  • pain (Payment Initiation) — pain.001 customer credit transfer, pain.002 status report, pain.008 customer direct debit.
  • pacs (Payment Clearing & Settlement) — pacs.008 customer credit transfer, pacs.009 financial-institution credit transfer, pacs.002 status, pacs.004 payment return, pacs.028 status request.
  • camt (Cash Management) — camt.052 intraday account report, camt.053 end-of-day statement, camt.054 debit/credit notification, camt.056 request for recall, camt.029 resolution of investigation, camt.110 / camt.111 case management for fraud.
  • acmt (Account Management) — Account opening, maintenance.
  • admi (Administrative) — System notifications between scheme participants.

3.5 Operational characteristics that distinguish engines

  • Throughput — UPI demands 100k+ TPS at peak; FedNow, FPS, SEPA Instant typically tens of thousands; cross-border far less but with stricter controls.
  • Latency — instant rails require sub-second engine processing; high-value rails tolerate more.
  • Availability — instant rails are 24×7×365; the engine and every dependency must be too.
  • Multi-tenancy — Payments-as-a-Service vendors (Form3, ClearBank) need strong tenant isolation; bank-internal hubs need legal-entity separation.
  • Configurability vs code — most engines blend a rules engine (Drools, Camunda DMN, in-house) with code; the more business-rules-as-config, the faster the change cadence.

4. Region by region — engines, rails, regulators, current as of 2025 Intermediate

4.1 India — UPI is the world’s template

India runs the world’s highest-volume retail payments rail. UPI processed 16+ billion transactions in October 2024, peaking at 500+ million per day. NPCI is the scheme operator; member banks plug in via NPCI-defined APIs. Major engines: Infosys Finacle Payments, TCS BaNCS Payments, ACI, Nucleus PaySe for the PSU banks; in-house engines at HDFC, ICICI; Mindgate is a major UPI infrastructure vendor behind many bank deployments. RBI Storage of Payment Data (April 2018) requires the entire payments-data lifecycle stored in India. UPI also interoperates with Singapore PayNow (2023), UAE AANI (2024), and several ASEAN rails through Project Nexus (BIS Innovation Hub).

4.2 United States — FedNow alongside Fedwire, ACH, CHIPS

  • FedNow live since 24 July 2023; 1,000+ participating financial institutions by end of 2024. Run by the Federal Reserve.
  • Fedwire migrated to ISO 20022 on 14 March 2025; CHIPS migrated April 2024.
  • ACH Same Day expanded to 3 windows in 2021; cap raised to USD 1M per transaction in March 2022.
  • RTP (Real-Time Payments) by The Clearing House (TCH), live since 2017; private-sector instant rail; coexists with FedNow.
  • Engine market: ACI Worldwide, Fiserv, FIS dominate the US bank market; Volante and Form3 growing; many regionals on FedNow via FedLine direct without a payments hub.

4.3 United Kingdom — modernisation of FPS, CHAPS, Bacs

  • CHAPS migrated to ISO 20022 in June 2023; Pay.UK and Bank of England are aligned.
  • Faster Payments New Payments Architecture (NPA) programme is rebuilding the central FPS infrastructure; engine vendors are aligning their platforms accordingly.
  • Confirmation of Payee (CoP) mandatory for the UK’s major banks; smaller PSPs joining through 2024–25.
  • UK PSR mandatory APP fraud reimbursement since October 2024; engines must score and decide in seconds.
  • Engine market: Form3 at Lloyds, Nationwide, Mettle, ClearBank; ACI heritage at HSBC, Barclays; Bottomline at building societies and many corporates.

4.4 Eurozone — SEPA Instant becomes mandatory

  • EU Instant Payments Regulation (IPR) — adopted 2024; receive obligation 9 January 2025, send obligation 9 October 2025; SEPA Instant must be priced no higher than regular SCT.
  • Verification of Payee (VoP) — EU equivalent of CoP; mandatory by 9 October 2025 for euro-area PSPs.
  • Target2 / TIPS / EURO1 all on ISO 20022 since March 2023.
  • Engine market: Volante (BNY Mellon, Standard Chartered EU), Finastra (Société Générale, Crédit Agricole, ICBC London), ACI, Temenos.

4.5 Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia, Brazil

  • Singapore — FAST + PayNow; MAS-led; PayNow interoperability with India UPI since February 2023, with Thailand, Malaysia in pilot.
  • Hong Kong — FPS + CHATS; HKMA-operated; FPS interoperability with Thailand PromptPay; cross-border QR with Mainland UnionPay and Alipay.
  • Australia — NPP + PayID; PayTo (equivalent of UK Variable Recurring Payments) live since 2022 and growing 2024–25; Mastercard is the scheme operator for NPP.
  • Brazil — PIX; BCB-operated, instant; now the dominant retail rail in Brazil with hundreds of millions of users.
  • Cross-border: Project Nexus (BIS Innovation Hub) standardises instant-payment connections between domestic rails; live phases through 2025–27 with India UPI, Singapore PayNow, Malaysia DuitNow, Thailand PromptPay, Philippines InstaPay.

4.6 Cross-border — SWIFT GPI, CBPR+, and the alternatives

  • SWIFT GPI (Global Payments Innovation) — launched 2017; brings tracking (UETR), transparency and time-bound settlement to cross-border. Now the default service level.
  • CBPR+ (Cross-Border Payments and Reporting Plus) — SWIFT’s ISO 20022 cross-border programme; co-existence with MT ends 22 November 2025.
  • SWIFT Go — low-value cross-border with predictable cost.
  • Alternatives — Wise Platform, Visa Direct, Mastercard Move, Ripple Payments, Banking Circle, Currencycloud, Thunes; competitors and partners to traditional correspondent banking.

5. Advanced — reference architecture, liquidity, recon, AI ops, CBDC Advanced

5.1 Inside the engine — the modern reference architecture

A modern payments hub looks roughly the same across vendors:

  • Channel adapters — REST / gRPC / file / host-to-host / Open Banking PIS endpoints.
  • Canonical message model — internal format (often ISO 20022-aligned) into which everything is normalised.
  • Validation and enrichment services — schema, IBAN / BIC / account checksum, beneficiary masterdata, FX rate, purpose code.
  • Sanctions and watchlist — Accuity World-Check, Dow Jones Risk & Compliance; engines embed or call via API.
  • Fraud and APP screening — Feedzai, NICE Actimize, FICO Falcon, BioCatch, Featurespace; Confirmation / Verification of Payee.
  • AML / transaction monitoring — real-time and post-event hooks.
  • Limits engine — customer / corporate / scheme / regulatory limits; quota management for instant rails.
  • Routing engine — picks the rail (cheapest, fastest, mandatory) based on amount, currency, urgency, beneficiary country.
  • Format translation — to scheme-specific dialect (MT, MX, NACHA, NACH, Bacstel-IP).
  • Connectivity — SWIFT Alliance Access / Cloud, FedLine, EBA Clearing, NPCI APIs, scheme-specific gateways.
  • Settlement and reconciliation — posting to core; nostro / vostro reconciliation; investigations.
  • Reporting — regulatory (MiFID II, EMIR, AnaCredit, MAS, RBI), customer (camt.053 / 054), and operational dashboards.

5.2 Liquidity and intraday cash management

Instant rails are 24×7. The bank’s liquidity must be available 24×7 too. Modern engines integrate with an intraday liquidity manager (Bottomline, Smartstream, Volante, Vermeg) to forecast and shape liquidity through the day. Concepts:

  • Pre-funding at FedNow / Target2 / RTGS-India; balances held with the central bank.
  • Liquidity bridges — automated movement between accounts (Target2 ↔ TIPS in the EU).
  • Throttling when liquidity is tight; queueing rules for high-value vs instant.
  • BCBS 248 intraday liquidity reporting — Basel monitoring tools; central banks watch.

5.3 Reconciliation, investigations and exceptions

  • Three-way recon — what we sent, what the rail confirms, what posted to the ledger.
  • Nostro reconciliation — account statements from correspondent banks matched against expected entries; SmartStream TLM is the dominant vendor.
  • Investigations — recall (camt.056), rejection (pacs.004), resolution (camt.029); case-management UIs in NICE Actimize, Pelican AI, in-house tools.
  • Repair and exception STP — automation of fixes via rules + AI for messages that fail validation; classic ROI driver in payments modernisation.

5.4 Tokenisation, encryption, key management

  • PCI DSS 4.0 — payment-card data tokenised wherever possible; vault providers Voltage / Protegrity / CipherTrust.
  • HSM-backed signing for SWIFT, RTGS, and instant-rail messages; FIPS 140-3 Level 3 typical.
  • Key custody — BYOK for cloud-managed deployments; HYOK / EKM where regulator-required.

5.5 AI and the payments operations function

  • Hit handling — sanctions and AML alerts triaged by Generative AI assistants; human reviewer remains in the loop.
  • Narrative enrichment — LLMs translate unstructured remittance text into structured ISO 20022 fields.
  • Fraud explanation — model outputs annotated to support reason codes for customers and regulators.
  • Operations copilot — agentic flows for repair, cross-system reconciliation, and root-cause analysis.

5.6 CBDC, stablecoins and the next rail

  • India e₹ — RBI retail and wholesale Central Bank Digital Currency pilots ongoing.
  • Project mBridge — multi-CBDC platform with HKMA, Bank of Thailand, CBUAE, PBOC, Saudi Central Bank.
  • ECB Digital Euro — preparation phase since November 2023; decision expected 2025.
  • Stablecoin payments — USDC and USDT rails for cross-border B2B; the EU MiCA regulation in force from June 2024 brings legal clarity in the EU.
  • Engine implications — engines must abstract ‘the rail’ enough that adding a CBDC or a stablecoin rail is a configuration, not a re-write.

6.2 Choosing an engine — the eight-question framework

  1. Which rails do we need on day 1, day 365, day 1,825?
  2. What is our peak transactions-per-second target on the highest-volume rail?
  3. What ISO 20022 readiness is the vendor offering for CBPR+, Fedwire, CHAPS, Target2, RTGS?
  4. How configurable is the rules engine vs how much requires the vendor to deliver a release?
  5. What is the deployment model — single-tenant containerised, vendor-cloud, on-prem? What is the regulator-acceptable model in our jurisdiction?
  6. What is the CoP / VoP / fraud / AML integration story?
  7. What is the upgrade cadence and the back-compat record?
  8. Who else in our peer group is on this engine, and what did their last upgrade cost in time and money?

6.3 Implementation partner choice

Most BFSI engine implementations are delivered with a global SI: HCLTech, TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Capgemini, Accenture, Cognizant, IBM Consulting, Atos / Eviden. The choice often follows existing portfolio relationships; ensure the chosen partner has practical references on the specific engine and rail you need.

7. Worked examples — five real BFSI payments-engine journeys Intermediate

Worked example — Goldman Sachs Transaction Banking on Volante
  1. Goldman launched Transaction Banking (TxB) in 2020 as a greenfield digital cash-management platform.
  2. Built on Volante VolPay-as-a-Service for payments processing across SWIFT, Fedwire, CHAPS, ACH; deployed on AWS.
  3. Lessons: greenfield + cloud-native engine + global rails launched in under three years; the engine is the productisable piece, not the legacy ledger.
Worked example — Lloyds Banking Group on Form3 for Faster Payments
  1. Lloyds adopted Form3’s Payments-as-a-Service for Faster Payments processing — believed to be the first tier-1 UK bank to use a fully-managed cloud Payments-as-a-Service for a primary rail.
  2. Form3 runs on AWS with active-active CockroachDB across multiple regions for resilience.
  3. Mettle (NatWest) and Nationwide also Form3 customers; ClearBank built its own cloud-native engine in parallel.
Worked example — An Indian PSU bank on TCS BaNCS Payments + UPI
  1. Tier-2 PSU bank runs TCS BaNCS Core, with TCS BaNCS Payments as the hub for RTGS, NEFT, IMPS, and a separate Mindgate-based UPI switch.
  2. RBI Storage of Payment Data keeps everything in Mumbai / Hyderabad regions of an Indian hyperscaler.
  3. Sanctions screening with Accuity World-Check; fraud monitoring with NICE Actimize; case management with in-house tooling.
  4. ISO 20022 readiness for CBPR+ achieved in 2024 in time for the November 2025 cutover.
Worked example — Standard Chartered EU on Volante and Finastra
  1. Standard Chartered runs Volante for some cross-border SWIFT and Finastra Global PAYplus for specific corridors; in-house components handle the channel layer.
  2. Standardised on Azure for the cloud-native components; HCLTech is one of the partners delivering modernisation work.
  3. ISO 20022 cutover for CBPR+ completed across all active corridors by mid-2025.
Worked example — FedNow rollout at a US regional bank
  1. Tier-2 US regional decided in 2023 to participate in FedNow as a receive-only participant first.
  2. Connected via FedLine Solution and used existing core (Fiserv DNA) with a thin Fiserv NOW! engine for ISO 20022 message handling.
  3. Year 2: enabled send-side; raised limits; integrated with Fiserv-supplied fraud screening.
  4. Public lessons: instant-rail liquidity management is the unfamiliar piece; treasury operations redesigned to support 24×7 funding.

8. Questions a leader asks in any payments-engine review Basic

  1. Which rails do we participate in today, and which are we obligated to add over the next 24 months under regulator timelines (IPR, NPA, FedNow, CBPR+)?
  2. What is our peak TPS per rail today and our forecast 12 / 24 / 36 months out?
  3. How many distinct engines do we operate, and what is the consolidation roadmap?
  4. Are we ISO 20022-native end to end, or do we still downgrade messages somewhere internally?
  5. What is our Confirmation / Verification of Payee coverage on outbound and inbound payments?
  6. Where do sanctions / AML / fraud sit in the flow, and what is the time budget per check?
  7. How do we manage 24×7 liquidity for the instant rails, and have we tested end-of-quarter / FX-volatility scenarios?
  8. What is our exception / repair STP rate, and what is the operations FTE cost per million payments?
  9. What is our recovery position if the engine fails — RTO, RPO, manual fallback documented and rehearsed?
  10. What is our exit plan from each Payments-as-a-Service vendor, per DORA / RBI / PRA expectations?
  11. Are upgrades and scheme-mandate windows integrated into our release calendar, with traceability to regulator deadlines?
  12. Are we ready operationally and from an engine perspective for CBDC and stablecoin rails when they go live?

9. Red flags + topic glossary Basic

Red flag — push back if you hear this
  • ‘We have one engine for everything.’ — Possible at mid-tier; rare at tier-1. Most large banks run 2–4 engines aligned to rail-family or geography; pretending otherwise hides risk.
  • ‘ISO 20022 is just a format change.’ — Wrong. The data is richer, the operations are different, and downstream systems must use the data; otherwise the migration is wasted.
  • ‘CoP / VoP is a nice-to-have.’ — In the UK and EU, it is mandatory and tied to APP fraud reimbursement obligations.
  • ‘Our engine is cloud-native because it runs in a VM in the cloud.’ — Cloud-native means containerised, horizontally scalable, observability-first, ISO 20022-native. Lift-and-shift is not modernisation.
  • ‘We outsourced to a Payments-as-a-Service vendor; compliance is theirs.’ — The bank remains accountable. DORA, RBI, PRA all require an exit plan and oversight.
  • ‘Sanctions screening on the file boundary is enough.’ — For instant rails, screening must be in-line with sub-second latency. File-boundary screening fits ACH / Bacs only.
  • ‘Liquidity is treasury’s problem, not the engine’s.’ — On instant rails, the engine is the eyes and ears of the treasurer. Integration must be tight.
  • ‘FedNow is just RTP with a different name.’ — Different operator (Fed vs TCH), different liquidity model, different participant onboarding. Both can coexist; many large banks support both.
  • ‘CBDC won’t affect us for years.’ — India e₹, ECB Digital Euro preparation, Project mBridge, mainland China e-CNY, MAS retail CBDC trial — engines need to be rail-agnostic now.
  • ‘Manual repair is fine, our STP rate is 95%.’ — At instant volumes 5% manual is unfeasible. The right answer is automation + AI exception handling, not more operations FTEs.

Glossary for this topic

  • Scheme — The rule book and central infrastructure of a payment system (UPI, SEPA, FPS, FedNow, SWIFT).
  • Rail — The network and message family used to move money on a scheme.
  • Engine / hub — Bank-side software that prepares, controls, routes and reconciles payments across rails.
  • ISO 20022 — Global standard for structured financial messages; pacs / pain / camt / acmt / admi families.
  • CBPR+ — Cross-Border Payments and Reporting Plus — SWIFT ISO 20022 cross-border programme; cutover 22 November 2025.
  • MT vs MX — Legacy SWIFT MT (free-text fields) vs modern SWIFT MX (ISO 20022 XML).
  • UETR — Unique End-to-End Transaction Reference — SWIFT GPI tracking identifier.
  • RTGS / DNS / Net — Real-Time Gross Settlement / Deferred Net Settlement / Net settlement models.
  • Fedwire / FedNow / CHIPS / ACH / RTP — Major US rails operated by the Fed and The Clearing House.
  • CHAPS / FPS / Bacs — Major UK rails.
  • SEPA SCT / SCT Inst / SDD — European credit transfer, instant credit transfer, direct debit.
  • Target2 / TIPS / EURO1 — Euro-area RTGS, instant, and net-settlement systems.
  • RTGS-India / NEFT / IMPS / UPI / NACH — Indian rails.
  • FAST / PayNow / NPP / PayID / PIX / FPS-HK / CHATS / AANI — Other major instant or RTGS rails.
  • Project Nexus — BIS Innovation Hub initiative linking domestic instant rails.
  • CoP / VoP — Confirmation of Payee (UK) / Verification of Payee (EU); validate beneficiary name before payment.
  • APP fraud — Authorised Push Payment fraud; UK mandatory reimbursement since October 2024.
  • EU Instant Payments Regulation (IPR) — Adopted 2024; mandates SEPA Instant; receive Jan 2025, send Oct 2025.
  • PCI DSS 4.0 — Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard; March 2025 fully in force.
  • SWIFT GPI — Global Payments Innovation; tracking and transparency for cross-border.
  • CBDC / mBridge / e₹ / Digital Euro — Central Bank Digital Currency programmes worldwide.
  • Stablecoin — Token pegged to fiat (USDC, USDT); MiCA regulation in EU since June 2024.
  • Volante / Form3 / ACI / Finastra / FIS / Fiserv / Temenos / Bottomline — Major payments-engine vendors.
  • Mindgate / Infosys Finacle Payments / TCS BaNCS Payments / IntellectAI iGTB / Pelican AI — Indian / APAC-leaning payments-engine vendors.
  • SmartStream TLM — Dominant nostro and exception-reconciliation platform.
  • BCBS 248 — Basel intraday liquidity monitoring tools.
