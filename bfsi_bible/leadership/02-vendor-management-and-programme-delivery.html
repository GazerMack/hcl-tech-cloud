VII.2 · Vendor management and large-programme delivery — RFP, MSA, SoW, governance, rituals

Most BFSI technology spend goes through vendors — hyperscalers, software vendors, system integrators, advisory firms, niche specialists. Most large programmes are delivered by a mix of internal teams and external partners under multi-year contracts. The skill that separates senior techno-functional leaders is not writing code or even drafting architecture — it is running these vendor and programme relationships well. This topic gives you fluent vocabulary in the contract stack (MSA, SoW, DPA, BAA, SLA), the procurement rituals (RFI, RFP, RFQ, demo, PoC, SOW), the delivery rituals (steerco, working group, RAID, scrum-of-scrums), and the regulator overlays (DORA CTP, RBI Outsourcing of IT Services, PRA SS2/21, MAS Outsourcing, NYDFS) that shape every contract.

0. Primer — vendors, contracts and what makes BFSI different Basic

Primer — read this even if you think you know it

A senior BFSI technology leader spends a quarter to half of their time on vendor relationships and programme delivery. The remaining time spent on engineering, architecture, hiring and strategy depends on these going well. Lose a programme to drift; let a vendor relationship sour; miss a regulator-mandated deadline — and the rest of the agenda stops mattering.

0.1 IT-side anchor — building a house with a contractor

From what you already know — your laptop

If you’ve ever built or renovated a house, you have done vendor management. You wrote a brief (RFP), got quotes (RFP responses), picked a builder (vendor selection), signed a contract (MSA + Statement of Work), agreed milestones (delivery plan), held weekly site visits (steerco), kept a list of issues (RAID log), paid against milestones (invoicing), and argued about variations (change requests). A USD 100M IT programme is the same shape, with more lawyers and a regulator.

0.2 BFSI-side anchor — what makes BFSI vendor management distinct

From what you already know — your bank account

Compared with non-regulated industries, BFSI vendor management has four extra constraints. Outsourcing rules (RBI, PRA, MAS, EU EBA, DORA) require board-approved policies, due diligence, exit plans, audit rights. Data localisation (RBI, China PIPL, Russia, increasingly Indonesia, Vietnam, Saudi) constrains where the vendor can host. Concentration risk on a small number of cloud and software vendors is now an explicit regulator topic. Operational resilience expects you to test what happens if a vendor goes down. None of these are optional, and all of them shape the contract.

0.3 The five vendor categories you must recognise

  • Hyperscalers and infrastructure — AWS, Azure, GCP, OCI, IBM Cloud, Alibaba; sometimes Equinix, Digital Realty, NTT for colocation.
  • Software product vendors — Temenos, Finacle, FIS, Fiserv, Murex, Calypso (Adenza), Guidewire, Duck Creek, Sapiens, ICE, Black Knight, Bloomberg, Aladdin, Snowflake, Databricks, Salesforce, Workday, ServiceNow.
  • System integrators (SIs) — HCLTech, TCS, Infosys, Wipro, Tech Mahindra, LTIMindtree, Mphasis, Cognizant, Capgemini, Accenture, IBM Consulting, Atos / Eviden, Kyndryl, NTT DATA, Deloitte, EY, KPMG, PwC.
  • Niche specialists — fintech platforms (Mambu, Thought Machine, Form3), security vendors (CrowdStrike, Wiz, Sigstore, Vault), data vendors (Bloomberg, LSEG, S&P, Moody’s).
  • Advisory firms — McKinsey, BCG, Bain, Oliver Wyman, Promontory; the strategic advisors who shape decisions before procurement starts.

0.4 The contract stack you will see on every deal

  • MSA — Master Services Agreement. Umbrella terms: liabilities, IP, data, audit rights, termination, dispute resolution.
  • SoW — Statement of Work. The specific scope, deliverables, milestones, acceptance criteria, fees per engagement.
  • DPA — Data Processing Agreement. Required under GDPR / UK GDPR / DPDP Act / state privacy laws; defines processor / controller responsibilities.
  • BAA — Business Associate Agreement. Healthcare-equivalent (US HIPAA); sometimes invoked for health-insurance lines.
  • SLA / SLO schedule. Measurable service levels, credits and remedies.
  • Security and resilience schedules. Vendor cyber controls, incident notification, TLPT cooperation; mandatory under DORA.
  • Exit plan / termination assistance schedule. How you get out, what data is returned, in what format, over what period.
  • Audit rights schedule. Who can audit, when, with what notice.

1. Why vendor management is now board-level Basic

Why is vendor management now a board-level technology topic?

  1. DORA designated CTPs. The EU Commission’s 2025 process formally designates Critical ICT Third Parties (hyperscalers and major vendors); they are subject to direct EU oversight; banks are required to hold tested exit plans.
  2. UK CTP regime live. Under FSMA 2023, HMT and the regulators jointly designate CTPs and set rules; first wave of designations expected during 2025.
  3. RBI Outsourcing of IT Services MD (April 2023). Comprehensive rule covering due diligence, board oversight, exit, and material outsourcing reporting.
  4. PRA SS2/21 + BoE / FCA / PRA OpRes — material outsourcing register, scenario testing, exit-plan reviews.
  5. MAS Outsourcing Guidelines + Cloud Annex; HKMA Supervisory Policy Manual SA-2; NYDFS Part 500.11 third-party service-provider security; OCC Heightened Standards + Fed SR 23-4.
  6. Talent + cost. Total cost of vendor relationships at tier-1 banks is regularly USD 5–15B a year; small efficiency gains are very large numbers.

3. How procurement, contracting and delivery actually work Intermediate

3.1 RFI / RFP / RFQ — the three procurement instruments

  • RFI (Request for Information) — early-stage market scan; ‘who exists, what do you do, ballpark price?’ Used to shortlist 3–6 vendors.
  • RFP (Request for Proposal) — formal scope, requirements, evaluation criteria; vendors respond with capability statements, references, demos, indicative pricing. The decision artefact.
  • RFQ (Request for Quotation) — used when scope is settled and price is the variable; common for hardware and commodity services.
  • PoC (Proof of Concept) — short, scoped, time-boxed test against your real data; typically 6–12 weeks; often paid; the most reliable input to a decision.

3.2 Vendor due diligence — what regulators expect to see

  • Financial health — audited accounts, going-concern view, parent-company support letters where relevant.
  • Cyber posture — SOC 2 Type II, ISO 27001, ISO 22301, PCI DSS attestations as applicable; penetration-test summaries; SBOM.
  • Operational resilience — capacity and redundancy, continuity tests, incident history.
  • Data and privacy — data-flow map, sub-processors, hosting locations, encryption-in-use approach, key custody.
  • Concentration and sub-contracting — who else uses them, what % of their revenue you are, their own dependencies (often other hyperscalers).
  • Conduct and integrity — sanctions and AML screening on the legal entity and beneficial owners; modern-slavery and ESG declarations.
  • Reference checks — three regulator-relevant peers.

3.3 MSA, SoW, DPA — the named clauses to negotiate

  • Liability cap — usually 1–3× annual fees; carve-outs for data breach, IP infringement, wilful misconduct, regulatory fines.
  • Indemnities — IP, third-party data, regulator fines arising from vendor act/omission.
  • Audit rights — bank, regulator and their appointees; reasonable notice; cost allocation.
  • Sub-contracting and assignment — vendor cannot sub-contract material services or assign without consent.
  • Change control — process, fee structure, dispute resolution.
  • Step-in / termination assistance — regulator may require step-in rights; vendor supports orderly exit at agreed rate-card.
  • Pricing — fixed, time-and-materials, capacity-based, outcome-based; indexation; benchmarking clauses.
  • Service credits — meaningful (e.g. >5% of monthly fee for material breaches), but credits are not the regulator’s only remedy.
  • Exit terms — notice, cost, data return format and timeline, parallel-running support.

3.4 Pricing models — pick by the work, not by habit

  • Fixed price / fixed scope — best when scope is genuinely settled; vendor carries delivery risk.
  • Time and materials (T&M) — for discovery, advisory, exploratory work; rate cards.
  • Managed services — agreed scope at agreed SLAs for monthly fee.
  • Capacity-based / FTE pool — common for SI delivery teams; ‘pod’ pricing.
  • Consumption / pay-as-you-go — hyperscalers; SaaS commitments and Reserved Instances; FinOps essential.
  • Outcome-based — fees tied to agreed outcomes (savings, throughput, NPS); intellectually attractive, operationally hard.

3.5 Programme delivery — the named rituals

  • Programme charter — purpose, scope, governance, sponsors, success criteria, constraints. Signed before money flows.
  • RAID log — Risks, Assumptions, Issues, Dependencies. Living document.
  • Steering committee (steerco) — monthly; SVP sponsor + business + risk + compliance + technology + vendor.
  • Working groups — weekly per stream; the engine room.
  • Scrum-of-scrums / Nexus / SAFe Release Train — operational coordination across teams.
  • Programme MIS — burn-down, burn-up, milestone tracker, RAG status, run-cost tracker, regulator-deadline tracker.
  • Quality gates — design, security, performance, regulator readiness; named owners.
  • Cutover and hypercare — go-live checklist, war-room, hypercare period.
  • Lessons learned — at the end of every release; a programme-level PIR.

3.6 RAG status — the most-abused report in BFSI

Red, Amber, Green status is the standard programme summary. The discipline is to keep the definition consistent: Green = on plan, no intervention needed; Amber = intervention required, recovery plan exists; Red = material risk to objective, executive escalation needed. Programmes that go from ‘green’ to ‘red’ in a single update without ‘amber’ in between have governance problems, not delivery problems.

4.2 Concentration risk — the conversation no one wants to have

Tier-1 banks have legitimate, large concentrations on a small number of vendors: AWS or Azure; one of HCLTech / TCS / Infosys / Accenture for application services; Murex or Aladdin for critical workflows; Bloomberg for data. Regulators are not against this, but they are against it being unacknowledged. The expected shape:

  • Documented identification of concentrations (vendor, service, geography, sub-processor).
  • Quantified impact tolerance per important business service.
  • Multi-year roadmap for de-concentration where possible (e.g. multi-cloud strategic capabilities).
  • Tested exit plan even where exit is implausible.

4.3 Multi-vendor and multi-cloud — when, and when not

  • Multi-vendor for SI services — common; encourages competition and avoids single-supplier capture.
  • Multi-cloud strategic — chosen deliberately for redundancy of business services, not for every workload; cost overhead is real.
  • Multi-cloud incidental — most tier-1 banks have workloads on 2–3 hyperscalers without it being a strategic choice; manage as estate, not architecture.
  • Single-vendor by design — acceptable when the workload is non-systemic and the exit plan is feasible.

5. Advanced — segmentation, failure modes, patterns, AI Advanced

5.1 Vendor segmentation — the four-quadrant view

  • Strategic — high spend + high criticality. Joint roadmap, executive sponsors, quarterly business reviews.
  • Operational — high spend + lower criticality. Disciplined commercial management, clear SLAs.
  • Critical — lower spend + high criticality. Often niche specialists; risk-managed tightly; succession plans.
  • Tactical — lower spend + lower criticality. Streamlined procurement; minimum compliance overhead.

5.2 Programme delivery — the eight failure modes

  • Scope creep without re-baseline — scope grows, plan stays; collision delayed not averted.
  • Vendor optimism in proposals — ‘we’ve done this 50 times’; rarely with this stack, this regulator, this scale.
  • Asymmetric incentives — vendor paid for activity, bank values outcomes; align before signing.
  • Discovery treated as delivery — the first 90 days are not the next 9 months.
  • Hero leadership — programme depends on one person; bus-factor 1.
  • Underweight risk and compliance — find regulator gaps in UAT, not design.
  • Steerco theatre — green RAG until the day before launch.
  • Hypercare absent — go-live with no war-room and no rollback plan.

5.3 Patterns that consistently work

  • Discovery sprint — 4–8 weeks before contract signature; co-funded if needed.
  • Two-vendor PoC — pay both, evaluate seriously, cheaper than a wrong decision.
  • Outcome-linked milestones — release fees against acceptance, not progress.
  • Embedded risk and compliance in the squad, not at the gate.
  • Independent assurance — small, named, standing review; not a dotted line on the org chart.
  • Programme war-room for cutover with named on-call across vendor, bank and regulator-relevant teams.
  • Lessons-learned culture — blameless write-ups; cross-programme libraries.

5.4 AI in vendor management and programme delivery

  • Contract analysis — Generative AI extracts obligations from MSAs and SoWs; named vendors include Ironclad, LinkSquares, Robin AI.
  • RAID and steerco summarisation — LLMs surface what changed since last steerco from Jira / ADO / spreadsheets.
  • Vendor monitoring — automated evidence collection (SOC 2 reports, status dashboards, regulator filings) for continuous third-party risk.
  • Demand forecasting — AI for capacity planning across vendor pools.
  • Audit readiness — RAG over ADRs, SoWs, RAID and risk decisions for fast regulator responses.

6.3 Steerco design — small choices that matter

  • Sponsor in the room, not delegated.
  • Risk and compliance in the room, not informed.
  • RAG is a leading indicator, not a status.
  • Named decisions, owners, dates per item.
  • Consent agenda for routine; debate the 2–3 decisions worth debating.
  • Vendor partner sits at the table for material items; both sides own the outcome.

7. Worked examples — five real BFSI vendor and programme stories Intermediate

Worked example — TSB 2018 — what a bad cutover costs
  1. TSB Bank in the UK migrated 5M customers from Lloyds infrastructure to a new Sabadell-built platform on a single weekend in April 2018.
  2. Defects, capacity gaps and incident-management failures left services degraded for weeks; FCA + PRA fined TSB £48.7M in 2022.
  3. Lessons widely cited: insufficient end-to-end testing; over-confident cutover plan; vendor and bank governance not joined up; insufficient resilience capacity for first-day load; hypercare staffing miscalculated.
Worked example — Lloyds + Thought Machine — multi-year strangler-fig
  1. Lloyds invested in Thought Machine and is progressively migrating products to Vault — savings first, lending later.
  2. Programme governance combines Lloyds product owners with Thought Machine engineering and tier-1 SI delivery teams.
  3. Lessons: multi-year migrations succeed by narrow scope per release, intense governance, explicit retreat options.
Worked example — Standard Chartered ‘aXess’ — multi-vendor platform engineering
  1. SC built an internal developer platform on Azure (AKS) with Backstage, Argo CD, Datadog; delivery is multi-vendor with HCLTech among others.
  2. Steerco governance separates platform engineering from product delivery; consumption metrics drive funding.
  3. Lessons: the platform team is its own product team; vendors deliver against platform standards, not against parallel architectures.
Worked example — Indian PSU bank — RBI Outsourcing MD compliance
  1. Tier-1 PSU bank reviewed its IT outsourcing estate against the April 2023 RBI MD; identified material vs non-material vendors, rewrote SoW templates, added board-approved outsourcing policy and exit plans.
  2. Lessons: regulatory updates of this size are an opportunity to consolidate, not just a compliance exercise.
Worked example — EU bank preparing for DORA — vendor-side workstream
  1. EU bank classified all material ICT third parties; obtained DORA-aligned addenda from every one; re-tested exit plans for two tier-1 vendors; reported register to the national competent authority by deadline.
  2. Lessons: vendor cooperation on addenda is tractable when the deadline is real; the exit-plan tests are where most rework happens.

8. Questions a leader asks of any vendor or programme review Basic

  1. Is the vendor on our material outsourcing register, and is the rationale documented?
  2. What is the tested exit plan, and when did we last rehearse it?
  3. Where does this engagement sit in the four-quadrant (strategic / operational / critical / tactical) view?
  4. What is the named SMF / officer accountable for the vendor relationship?
  5. Is the SoW outcome-linked, time-and-materials, or fixed-price — and is that the right model for the work?
  6. What does the RAID log say is on fire this week, and who owns each item?
  7. Has the steerco moved from ‘green’ to ‘red’ without an ‘amber’ in between?
  8. What is the regulator-deadline tracker for this programme, and is there a credible margin to each deadline?
  9. What concentration are we creating with this engagement, and how is it acknowledged?
  10. Are security and resilience clauses (DORA-aligned) in the contract, with TLPT cooperation, incident notification and audit rights?
  11. What is the exit-cost projection, and have we modelled it?
  12. What are the 2–3 decisions we need to make at this steerco, and who is the decision-maker?

9. Red flags + topic glossary Basic

Red flag — push back if you hear this
  • ‘The vendor will tell us if there is a problem.’ — They will, eventually. Independent assurance and your own delivery insight catch it earlier.
  • ‘Fixed price is safer.’ — Fixed price is safer for scope you understand; it is more dangerous for scope you don’t. Match the model to the work.
  • ‘Service credits are our protection.’ — They are a tiny fraction of the loss in a real incident. The controls that prevent the incident are the protection.
  • ‘RFP scoring solves vendor selection.’ — Scoring rationalises a decision that has often already been made. The PoC and the references settle it.
  • ‘Exit plans are paperwork.’ — DORA tests them; RBI tests them; PRA tests them. Untested exit plans are findings.
  • ‘Programme is green.’ — Green for what? On budget, on scope, on time, on outcome — usually one is true and one is hiding.
  • ‘Steerco doesn’t need risk and compliance.’ — Then it isn’t a BFSI steerco.
  • ‘Sub-contracting was a vendor decision.’ — In BFSI regulators expect explicit consent for material sub-contracting.
  • ‘We’ll outsource the operational risk to the vendor.’ — Operational risk cannot be outsourced; only the activity can.
  • ‘We don’t need a programme war-room; the vendor has one.’ — You both need one. Cutover incidents compound across boundaries.

  • Sub-processor / sub-contractor — Vendor’s vendors; subject to bank consent for material services.

  • Right of audit — Bank, regulator and appointee audit rights baked into the contract.
  • Indemnity / liability cap — Risk-allocation clauses negotiated in MSAs.
  • Service credit — Fee reduction for SLA breaches; not the only remedy.
  • Discovery sprint — Pre-contract paid exploration to de-risk scope and design.
  • Strangler-fig migration — Incremental replacement pattern; default for legacy modernisation.
  • Programme PIR — Post-Implementation Review at the programme level.
