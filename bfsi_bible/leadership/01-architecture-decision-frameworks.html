VII.1 · Architecture decision frameworks for techno-functional leaders

Senior leaders in BFSI technology are not paid to write code; they are paid to make a small number of consequential decisions well, and then to defend them under audit, regulator scrutiny and market change. This topic gives you the named frameworks leaders use to make those decisions: ADRs and the lightweight documentation discipline; fitness functions for evolutionary architecture; Wardley mapping for strategic positioning; build-vs-buy-vs-partner; the TOGAF and BIAN reference models; the ‘capability map’ way of looking at a bank; and the rituals — ARBs, design authorities, principal-engineer reviews — that turn decisions into outcomes.

0. Primer — leaders make decisions, not designs Basic

Primer — read this even if you think you know it

Most architecture decisions a BFSI leader regrets in year three were made in a hurry in year one with no written rationale. The discipline is not bigger models; it is making the consequential decisions visible, reversible-where-possible, and tested in production-like conditions before they become irreversible.

0.1 IT-side anchor — the architect as a town planner

From what you already know — your laptop

Architects often imagine themselves as building designers. A more honest analogy is the town planner: deciding where roads, water, schools and zoning go, not what the houses look like. You don’t design every house; you decide the rules under which thousands of houses can be built without grinding traffic to a halt or flooding the drains. Capability maps are zoning. ADRs are planning permissions. Fitness functions are the noise and traffic limits.

0.2 BFSI-side anchor — the credit committee for technology

From what you already know — your bank account

Banks already know how to make consequential decisions: the credit committee. A loan is presented with a clear summary, an explicit risk rating, comparable cases, dissenting views, and a post-decision review schedule. Architecture decisions deserve exactly the same discipline. An ADR (Architecture Decision Record) is the credit memo for a technical decision. An ARB (Architecture Review Board) is the credit committee. The post-decision review is the same.

0.3 Five frameworks worth memorising

  • ADR — Architecture Decision Record. Lightweight markdown, version-controlled, one decision per file. Michael Nygard 2011.
  • Fitness functions. Automated, continuous tests of architectural properties. Neal Ford / Rebecca Parsons / Patrick Kua, Building Evolutionary Architectures.
  • Wardley mapping. Strategic positioning of components on a value-chain × evolution map. Simon Wardley.
  • TOGAF and BIAN. The Open Group Architecture Framework + Banking Industry Architecture Network — the BFSI reference models regulators and partners speak.
  • Capability mapping. A flat, verb-free map of what the bank does. Used to locate every technology investment against a stable business shape.

1. Why frameworks are the leadership job, not the engineering job Basic

Why does this topic deserve its own chapter rather than a footnote in IV.1?

  1. The cost of bad architecture decisions in BFSI is asymmetric. A poor microservice boundary at a fintech is annoying; at a tier-1 bank it is a 200-FTE programme. Small numbers of decisions drive most outcomes.
  2. Regulators now expect documented rationale. DORA, RBI IT Governance MD, MAS TRM, NYDFS Part 500 expect to see why a bank chose its critical-third-party vendor, not just that it did.
  3. AI is amplifying the consequences. An ML model embedded in credit, fraud or pricing is an architecture decision; the EU AI Act asks for the lifecycle paper trail.
  4. Talent attrition is real. Without ADRs the institution forgets why; the next leader rebuilds the same room.
  5. Cost discipline. Build-vs-buy and build-vs-partner choices made well save 9-figure sums over a decade.

3. How leaders use these frameworks day to day Intermediate

3.1 ADRs — Architecture Decision Records

An ADR is a short markdown file with a fixed shape:

  • Title and number (ADR-0042).
  • Status — proposed, accepted, deprecated, superseded by ADR-0083.
  • Context — what forces are at play; what we know; what we don’t.
  • Decision — what we will do, in one or two sentences.
  • Consequences — what becomes easier; what becomes harder; what is no longer true.

ADRs live in the source repository, beside the code; review happens in pull requests; status is owned by principal engineers. Tools: adr-tools (Nat Pryce), adr-manager, Backstage TechDocs, Structurizr (Simon Brown).

3.2 C4 model — communicate at four zoom levels

  • Level 1 — System Context: the system and the people / systems it interacts with.
  • Level 2 — Container: applications and data stores inside the system boundary.
  • Level 3 — Component: significant components inside a container.
  • Level 4 — Code: optional; class or module-level diagrams.

The C4 model (Simon Brown, 2018+) replaced UML in most BFSI shops because it has only four diagram types and is easy to keep up to date.

3.3 Fitness functions — architecture as code

A fitness function is an automated test of an architectural property. Examples used in practice in BFSI:

  • Every public API has an OpenAPI spec; CI fails if missing.
  • No service depends on more than 3 downstream services synchronously (limit fan-out).
  • P99 latency on the payments hub stays under 500 ms in production-like load.
  • Every container image carries an SBOM and a Sigstore signature.
  • No PII column in the data lake without a tag and a tokenisation status.
  • Cost per transaction trends downward week-on-week (FinOps fitness function).

Fitness functions live in CI / observability; failures are tickets, not opinions. Reference: Neal Ford et al., Building Evolutionary Architectures (2017, 2nd ed. 2023).

3.4 Wardley mapping — where on the lifecycle is each component?

A Wardley map plots components of a value chain on two axes: visibility to the user (vertical) and evolution stage (horizontal: Genesis → Custom-built → Product/Rental → Commodity / Utility). The discipline forces you to ask: ‘should this still be custom-built or is it now a utility?’ — the question that decides whether you build, buy or consume Postgres / K8s / observability.

3.6 Capability mapping — the bank as a flat list

A capability map says what the bank does in verb-free language: ‘Customer Onboarding’, ‘Counterparty Credit Assessment’, ‘Cash Management’, ‘Claims Adjudication’. Capabilities are stable for decades; processes, products and systems shift constantly. The map is the canvas onto which every investment, every regulator finding and every vendor is plotted. BIAN (Banking Industry Architecture Network) publishes a reference capability and service-domain map for banking; ACORD publishes the equivalent for insurance.

3.7 TOGAF, BIAN, ACORD — when the words matter

  • TOGAF (Open Group, currently TOGAF 10 / 2022) — generic enterprise architecture method (ADM); known as much for its certification as for its content; useful as a shared vocabulary.
  • BIAN — banking-specific service domains; the bank already speaks this when discussing vendors and APIs.
  • ACORD — insurance-specific vocabulary, message standards and capability maps.
  • IFW (IBM), Oracle Reference Models, Capgemini Sefas — vendor-specific reference architectures often used alongside.

3.8 ARB, design authority and principal-engineer reviews

  • Architecture Review Board (ARB) — monthly or fortnightly forum; decisions above a threshold (cost, regulator impact, cross-domain scope) need ARB approval; minutes are an audit artifact.
  • Design Authority — programme-level version of the ARB for a single change programme.
  • Principal-engineer / staff-plus reviews — peer review for technical depth; often the more useful of the two.
  • Pre-mortem — before commitment, ask ‘imagine this has failed in 18 months — what happened?’. Cheap, surprisingly effective.
  • Post-implementation review (PIR) — did the decision deliver the consequences we predicted? Feeds the ADR status.

4. Region by region — how banks run this and what regulators expect Intermediate

4.1 How banks worldwide actually run this

  • JPMorgan Chase — published Engineering Standards, golden paths, internal developer platform; ‘paved roads’ for cloud, Kubernetes, data; principal-engineer ladder is highly visible.
  • Goldman Sachs — Marquee + developer-platform discipline; SecDB heritage of ‘one platform for everything’ with explicit trade-offs.
  • Capital One — Cloud Custodian, Hygieia, public ADR-style engineering blog; early adopter of fitness-function thinking.
  • ING — squads / tribes / chapters model; design authorities at programme level.
  • Standard Chartered — ‘aXess’ developer platform, internal architecture council; HCLTech is one of several partners contributing components.
  • HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank, Axis Bank — internal architecture committees; recent build-out of ADR practice and capability maps; modernisation-board governance.

4.2 Regulator expectations on architecture governance

  • RBI IT Governance MD (Nov 2023) — expects ‘board-approved IT strategy and architecture’; ARBs and capability maps are a clean way to evidence this.
  • EU DORA Article 6 — ‘ICT risk management framework’ shall include the ‘ICT architecture’ and a clear assignment of responsibilities.
  • FCA / PRA — Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) means an SMF24 has personal accountability for ‘systems and controls’; documented architecture decisions help.
  • NYDFS Part 500.4 — CISO certification implies an ability to describe the architecture under oath.
  • MAS TRM Section 4.1 — board and senior management to approve a technology risk framework that includes architecture.

5. Advanced — patterns, anti-patterns, cost discipline, AI era Advanced

5.1 Anti-patterns to name and refuse

  • Architecture astronaut — diagrams without rationale; ADR-less decisions; ‘we’re going to standardise on a service mesh’ without a problem statement.
  • Resume-driven development — picking technology because it looks good on a CV. Common with shiny ML, blockchain, mesh tools.
  • Big-bang re-platform — ‘we’ll rewrite the core in three years’. Almost always extends to seven; strangler-fig is the default.
  • Vendor capture — sole-source decisions made informally; no ADR; no exit clause.
  • Decision by committee with no owner — ‘the ARB decided’ becomes ‘nobody decided’.
  • Premature standardisation — freezing a stack before the first three implementations have shaken out.
  • Architecture without operations — diagrams that ignore SRE, FinOps, identity, observability.

5.2 Patterns to embed and defend

  • Strangler-fig migrations — wrap, redirect, retire; never big-bang.
  • Two-pizza teams + bounded contexts — Conway’s Law respected on purpose.
  • Outcome-based SLOs — measure important business services, not ‘server availability’.
  • Crypto-agility — pluggable primitives; PQC migration becomes a configuration.
  • Data products with owners — data mesh discipline; SLAs on data quality and freshness.
  • Paved roads — golden paths in the Internal Developer Platform; teams can deviate but they own the cost.
  • Reversibility-first decisions — Bezos ‘two-way doors’; treat reversible decisions lightly, irreversible ones rigorously.

5.3 Cost-discipline patterns

  • Unit economics — cost per transaction, per customer, per API call. The FinOps fitness function.
  • Run-cost in design — budget for 5-year run cost when approving a build; show the envelope at the ARB.
  • Sunset budgets — every project must propose what it retires.
  • Vendor portfolio review — annual review of redundant or sub-scale vendors; conscious consolidation.

5.4 AI-era additions to the toolkit

  • Model registry as architecture asset — every model is a component of the architecture, not a side project.
  • Data lineage as a non-negotiable — OpenLineage, Unity Catalog, Marquez; auditors and AI agents both want it.
  • Prompt and evaluation governance — treat prompts and eval suites like code; ADRs for major prompt-strategy changes.
  • Human-in-the-loop is a requirement, not a nicety — under EU AI Act, FCA Consumer Duty, MAS FEAT, RBI guidance.

7. Worked examples — five real architecture-governance stories Intermediate

Worked example — Capital One — public-facing engineering practices
  1. Capital One open-sourced Cloud Custodian and Hygieia and published its engineering principles; its post-2019 breach reviews fed visible changes (IMDSv2, least-privilege IAM by default).
  2. Public lessons: visible principles + open tooling + explicit post-incident learnings make a culture you can hire into.
Worked example — JPM ‘paved road’ for cloud and data
  1. JPM publishes internal Engineering Standards; developer platform with ‘paved roads’ for AWS, Kubernetes, OpenSearch, Snowflake; deviations require an explicit ADR and exit plan.
  2. Lessons: paved roads + opt-out discipline scales 150,000 engineers; total platform standardisation is impossible at that size; explicit deviation is the answer.
Worked example — ING Tribes / Squads / Chapters
  1. ING reorganised IT around the Spotify-derived tribes / squads / chapters model from 2015 with design authorities at the programme level rather than centralised.
  2. Public lessons: distribute decisions to the domain; keep cross-cutting concerns in chapters (security, data, SRE).
Worked example — Indian incumbent — modernisation-board governance
  1. A tier-1 Indian private bank stood up a modernisation board with CTO + Chief Risk + Chief Compliance + business heads; reviewed all investments above ₹50 Cr.
  2. Adopted lightweight ADRs (markdown in GitHub Enterprise); ARB cadence monthly; PIR three months post-go-live.
  3. Lessons: senior cross-functional governance is the lever; tooling follows.
Worked example — EU bank for DORA — written architecture story
  1. EU bank prepared for DORA by writing a single ‘architecture story’: capability map, level-1 C4 of every important business service, ADR register, fitness-function dashboard, exit plan per critical ICT third party.
  2. Result: regulator review reduced from a 6-month discovery to a 6-week confirmation.

8. Questions a leader asks of any architecture review Basic

  1. Is the decision in writing as an ADR with status, context, decision, consequences?
  2. Have we plotted the relevant capabilities on the BIAN / ACORD / capability map and located the investment correctly?
  3. Where on the Wardley evolution axis is each component, and is build / buy / partner aligned?
  4. What fitness functions express the architectural properties we care about, and are they running in CI / observability?
  5. Is this a reversible (two-way door) or irreversible decision, and have we calibrated rigour accordingly?
  6. What is the exit plan from each critical vendor / cloud / SaaS in this scope?
  7. Who is the SMF / responsible officer, and is their name on the decision?
  8. How does the design respect Conway’s Law — does it match the team topology we have or the one we are willing to build?
  9. What is the run-cost projection over five years, and is FinOps embedded?
  10. What is the post-implementation review schedule, and what would change our minds?
  11. How does the design handle PQC migration, AI Act / FEAT obligations, and operational resilience scenarios?
  12. If a regulator walks in tomorrow and asks for the architecture, can we hand them one document?

9. Red flags + topic glossary Basic

Red flag — push back if you hear this
  • ‘We don’t need ADRs, the team knows the rationale.’ — The team will turn over by year three. Future leaders will inherit decisions they cannot defend.
  • ‘Our ARB approves everything.’ — A 100% approval rate is a signal the ARB is theatre.
  • ‘Fitness functions are nice-to-have.’ — Without them, architectural intent decays silently and is discovered in audits.
  • ‘Wardley maps are too abstract.’ — They take 90 minutes to learn and routinely change build/buy decisions worth millions.
  • ‘TOGAF / BIAN / ACORD are paperwork.’ — They are shared vocabulary with regulators and partners; use them lightly, but use them.
  • ‘We picked the vendor because they were the incumbent.’ — Without an ADR documenting the alternatives, this is failed governance under DORA / RBI / PRA.
  • ‘The ARB owns the decision.’ — A committee cannot be held to account. An individual SMF / officer must own each consequential decision.
  • ‘Diagrams are outdated, ignore them.’ — Then remove them. Stale diagrams are worse than no diagrams.
  • ‘AI doesn’t need architectural review.’ — Models are components; data flows are integrations; regulators are watching.
  • ‘Architecture is what the architect does.’ — Architecture is what the system is. The architect documents and shapes it.
