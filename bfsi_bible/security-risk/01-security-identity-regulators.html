V.1 · Security, identity, and the global regulator landscape

A bank is, in the end, a trust machine. Strip away the products and the channels and what remains is a promise that money in is money safe. Security and identity are how that promise is engineered; the regulator landscape is how it is enforced. This topic gives you a fluent vocabulary in IAM, cryptography and Hardware Security Modules (HSMs), zero trust, post-quantum cryptography (PQC), DevSecOps, and the regulator regimes that now dominate BFSI design reviews — DORA, NIS2, the EU AI Act, UK FCA Operational Resilience and the Critical Third Parties (CTP) regime, RBI Cyber Security Master Directions, NYDFS Part 500, MAS TRM, and HKMA C-RAF.

0. Primer — six disciplines, one trust promise Basic

Primer — read this even if you think you know it

Security in BFSI is not one discipline; it is six, working in concert. Identity decides who is acting; cryptography decides what they can read and trust; network and endpoint controls decide where they can act; application security decides whether the code they touch is trustworthy; security operations watches them; and the regulator decides what evidence you must produce to prove it. Get any of the six wrong and you have an incident.

0.1 IT-side anchor — the keys, the lock, and the doorbell

From what you already know — your laptop

Think of your front door. The key is authentication — proof you are you. The lock is authorisation — what the key opens (the front door, not the safe). The doorbell camera is logging and monitoring — a record of who came when. If you have a smart lock, the app on your phone is the identity provider that lets the lock and the camera and the alarm all agree about who you are. Banks have the same parts, on a vastly larger scale.

0.2 BFSI-side anchor — what you already do every login

From what you already know — your bank account

When you log in to your bank app, three things happen in under a second. Your fingerprint or face unlocks a key stored in the secure element of your phone. The phone presents that key to the bank as part of an OAuth 2.0 / OpenID Connect flow. The bank decides what you can do (view balance, transfer up to ₹1,00,000 without a second factor) using authorisation rules that take in your device, your location, the time of day and the amount. If anything looks unusual, a step-up authentication challenge is fired. That is Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) in the EU, Risk-Based Authentication in the United States, and two-factor everywhere else. You experience it as ‘the bank app asked me for my PIN again’; the engineer experiences it as a six-discipline decision under 200 milliseconds.

0.3 The six layers of a BFSI security stack

  • Identity and Access Management (IAM) — who is acting and what are they allowed to do? Workforce, customer, machine.
  • Cryptography and key management — what can be read, signed, decrypted; HSMs, KMS, certificates.
  • Network and endpoint security — where they can act from; segmentation, zero trust, EDR / XDR.
  • Application security and DevSecOps — is the code itself trustworthy; SAST, DAST, SCA, SBOM.
  • Security operations (SecOps) — watching and responding; SOC, SIEM, SOAR, threat intelligence, incident response.
  • Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC) — the policies, the regulators, the auditors; the evidence machine.

1. Why security is now the headline BFSI conversation Basic

Why is this topic now more board-level than it has ever been?

  1. Operational resilience is now law. EU DORA in force 17 January 2025; UK Operational Resilience fully embedded March 2025; UK Critical Third Parties regime active under FSMA 2023; RBI Cyber Security and IT Governance Master Directions (2023). Boards are now personally accountable for cyber posture.
  2. Ransomware and APP fraud at industrial scale. Authorised Push Payment fraud crossed £459M in the UK in 2023; mandatory reimbursement from October 2024 changes incentives sharply. Ransomware against banks and their third parties (ION 2023, MOVEit 2023, ICBC 2023) is now annual.
  3. The cryptographic horizon. NIST published the final Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) standards ML-KEM (FIPS 203), ML-DSA (FIPS 204), SLH-DSA (FIPS 205) in August 2024. Banks must inventory cryptography now to be migrated by the early-2030s deadline regulators are signalling.
  4. AI risk. EU AI Act in force August 2024; MAS FEAT principles; Singapore Model AI Governance Framework; NIST AI RMF; UK AISI; India DPDP + RBI supervisory expectations.
  5. Identity is the new perimeter. The network perimeter has dissolved; SaaS and mobile mean identity is the only consistent control point.
  6. Concentration risk. Hyperscalers, key vendors, payment rails — when one fails, many fail together. Regulators now treat this as a systemic-risk topic.

3. How the security stack actually works — six disciplines in detail Intermediate

3.1 Identity — workforce, customer, machine

  • Workforce identity — the bank’s employees, contractors and partners. Single Sign-On (SSO) via OIDC / SAML 2.0 against the corporate IdP (Microsoft Entra ID, Okta, Ping, ForgeRock). Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is mandatory; phishing-resistant MFA — FIDO2 security keys, Windows Hello, passkeys — is the modern target. Privileged Access Management (PAM) via CyberArk, BeyondTrust, Delinea, HashiCorp Boundary; just-in-time elevation is now the norm.
  • Customer identity (CIAM) — the bank’s retail and corporate customers. Custom in-house at most tier-1 banks; ForgeRock (Ping), Auth0 (Okta), Transmit Security, Akamai Identity Cloud are major commercial platforms. Federated to national identity in some markets — Aadhaar / e-KYC in India, SingPass in Singapore, BankID in the Nordics, itsme in Belgium, SPID in Italy, GOV.UK One Login in the UK.
  • Machine / workload identity — services, scripts, and pipelines. SPIFFE / SPIRE (CNCF) for cloud-neutral workload identity; AWS IAM Roles Anywhere, Azure Managed Identity, GCP Workload Identity Federation for cloud-native; HashiCorp Vault for short-lived credentials.

3.2 Authentication and the slow death of passwords

  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn / passkeys — public-key based, phishing-resistant. Apple, Google, Microsoft passkeys converging on a common UX. Banks rolling out to customers in 2024–2025.
  • Strong Customer Authentication (SCA) — PSD2 RTS requirement: two of three factors (knowledge, possession, inherence). Mandatory for online card payments and Open Banking transactions.
  • Risk-Based Authentication (RBA) — evaluate device, network, behaviour; step up only when risk crosses a threshold. BioCatch, Feedzai, BehavioSec, Transmit Security are common.
  • Step-up authentication — escalate to a stronger factor for higher-risk actions.
  • Confirmation of Payee (CoP) — UK innovation now spreading; verify the beneficiary name before payment to reduce APP fraud.

3.3 Authorisation — RBAC, ABAC, ReBAC, policy as code

  • Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) — the old default; users get roles, roles get permissions. Suffers role explosion at scale.
  • Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC) — decisions based on attributes (department, location, data classification, time, risk score). NIST SP 800-162. More expressive, harder to debug.
  • Relationship-Based Access Control (ReBAC) — Google Zanzibar paper (2019), now productised as SpiceDB, OpenFGA, Permify; powers fine-grained permissions in modern fintech.
  • Policy as code — Open Policy Agent (OPA) / Rego, Cedar (AWS), Kyverno; policies expressed in code, version-controlled, tested.
  • Just-in-time / break-glass — production access is granted on request, time-bound, recorded, and reviewed.

3.4 Cryptography — the moving floor

  • Symmetric — AES-256-GCM is the default for data encryption; ChaCha20-Poly1305 where AES-NI is absent.
  • Asymmetric (classical) — RSA-2048 and RSA-3072 still ubiquitous; Elliptic-Curve Cryptography (ECC) preferred for new systems (NIST P-256 / P-384, Curve25519).
  • Hashing — SHA-256, SHA-3-256; password hashing with Argon2id, scrypt, or bcrypt; never SHA-1 or MD5 for new work; HMAC-SHA-256 for message authentication.
  • Transport — TLS 1.3 is the modern baseline; TLS 1.2 acceptable; everything below deprecated. mTLS where both ends present certificates.
  • Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC) — NIST FIPS 203 ML-KEM (key encapsulation, ex-Kyber), FIPS 204 ML-DSA (signatures, ex-Dilithium), FIPS 205 SLH-DSA (hash-based signatures, ex-SPHINCS+) finalised August 2024. Banks are starting cryptographic inventories and hybrid TLS deployments now to be ready by the early-to-mid 2030s.

3.5 Key management and HSMs

  • Hardware Security Module (HSM) — tamper-resistant device that performs cryptographic operations and never releases the private key. Thales Luna, Entrust nShield, Utimaco, IBM Crypto Express (z/OS), AWS CloudHSM, Azure Key Vault Managed HSM, GCP Cloud HSM, OCI Vault HSM.
  • FIPS 140-3 Level 3 — the certification BFSI HSMs target; mandatory for many regulator controls.
  • Cloud KMS — fronts the HSM with a managed API; typical pattern is BYOK (Bring Your Own Key) or HYOK / EKM (Hold Your Own Key / External Key Manager) for sensitive workloads.
  • Key lifecycle — generation, rotation, revocation, archival, destruction; rotation cadence tied to the data classification.
  • Tokenisation and Format-Preserving Encryption (FPE) — replace card PANs and similar with tokens; Voltage SecureData, Protegrity, Thales CipherTrust, Comforte.
  • Confidential computing — AMD SEV-SNP, Intel TDX, AWS Nitro Enclaves, Azure Confidential VMs, GCP Confidential VMs; data encrypted while in use.

3.6 Public Key Infrastructure (PKI)

  • Internal PKI — Microsoft AD CS, HashiCorp Vault PKI, AWS Private CA, GCP CAS; signs internal mTLS certs, code-signing certs.
  • External PKI — DigiCert, Sectigo, GlobalSign, Entrust; commercial TLS certs.
  • eIDAS qualified PKI — Qualified Trust Service Providers (QTSPs) issue QWAC and QSealC certificates required by PSD2.
  • Certificate Lifecycle Management — Venafi, Keyfactor, AppViewX; automation to prevent expiry incidents (the recurring root cause of bank outages).

3.7 Network — zero trust, segmentation, SASE

The 2010 Forrester paper popularised Zero Trust (ZT): never trust, always verify. NIST SP 800-207 (2020) is the canonical reference. Operational shapes:

  • Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA) — replace VPN with identity-aware proxies. Cloudflare Access, Zscaler Private Access, Palo Alto Prisma, Netskope, Cisco Duo.
  • Secure Access Service Edge (SASE) — ZTNA + SD-WAN + Cloud Access Security Broker (CASB) + Secure Web Gateway (SWG) + Firewall-as-a-Service (FWaaS). The ‘network is a service’ model.
  • Microsegmentation — Illumio, Guardicore (Akamai), VMware NSX; identity-aware east-west firewalling.
  • DDoS protection — Cloudflare, Akamai, AWS Shield Advanced, Azure DDoS Protection, Imperva.

3.8 Endpoint, EDR / XDR, and the rise of SaaS security

  • EDR / XDR — CrowdStrike Falcon, SentinelOne, Microsoft Defender for Endpoint, Palo Alto Cortex XDR, Trend Micro.
  • Mobile Threat Defence (MTD) — Lookout, Zimperium; relevant for mobile-banking apps.
  • Browser security — Island, Talon (Palo Alto), Menlo; isolating SaaS access.
  • SaaS Security Posture Management (SSPM) — Adaptive Shield, AppOmni, Obsidian; auditing SaaS config (Salesforce, Microsoft 365, Workday).

3.9 Application security and DevSecOps

  • SAST (Static) — Checkmarx, Veracode, Sonatype, Snyk Code, GitHub Advanced Security, Semgrep.
  • DAST (Dynamic) — OWASP ZAP, Burp Suite Enterprise, Invicti, Veracode DAST.
  • SCA (Software Composition Analysis) — Snyk, Black Duck, Sonatype Nexus IQ, GitHub Dependabot.
  • API security — Salt Security, Noname, Traceable, Akamai API Security; OWASP API Top 10 (2023).
  • SBOM and supply-chain — CycloneDX / SPDX SBOMs, SLSA provenance, Sigstore (cosign) signing; required by EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) and US Executive Order 14028.
  • Secrets scanning — GitGuardian, TruffleHog, GitHub Push Protection.
  • Cloud Security Posture Management (CSPM), Cloud-Native Application Protection Platform (CNAPP) — Wiz, Prisma Cloud, Orca, Lacework, Microsoft Defender for Cloud.

3.10 Security operations — SOC, SIEM, SOAR, threat intel

  • SIEM — Splunk, Microsoft Sentinel, Google Chronicle / SecOps, Elastic Security, IBM QRadar, Sumo Logic.
  • SOAR — Cortex XSOAR (Palo Alto), Splunk SOAR, Tines, Torq, Swimlane; automating response runbooks.
  • Threat intelligence — Recorded Future, Mandiant (Google), Flashpoint, FS-ISAC for BFSI sector intel; STIX / TAXII as the exchange format.
  • Incident response — Mandiant, CrowdStrike Services, Kroll, Palo Alto Unit 42; retainers are standard for tier-1 banks.
  • Threat-Led Penetration Testing (TLPT) — TIBER-EU framework (ECB), CBEST (UK), iCAST (HKMA), AASE (MAS), GBEST (Saudi); now mandated by DORA for the most critical services.
  • Purple teaming — red and blue teams working together; MITRE ATT&CK as the lingua franca of adversary behaviour.

4. Region by region — the regulator landscape, current as of 2025 Intermediate

4.1 Eurozone — DORA, NIS2, AI Act, GDPR

  • DORA (Digital Operational Resilience Act) — in force 17 January 2025. ICT risk management, incident reporting, threat-led penetration testing, and oversight of critical ICT third parties (hyperscalers and major vendors).
  • NIS2 Directive — applies from October 2024; widens the cybersecurity baseline across ‘essential’ and ‘important’ entities; banks already covered, but vendors many banks rely on are now in scope.
  • EU AI Act — in force August 2024; phased applicability through August 2026. Risk-based classification; high-risk use cases include credit scoring and biometric verification.
  • EU Cyber Resilience Act (CRA) — adopted 2024; product-side cyber rules including SBOM and vulnerability disclosure for ‘products with digital elements’.
  • GDPR — still the foundation; UK GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 in the UK.
  • eIDAS 2.0 — adopted 2024; introduces the European Digital Identity Wallet from 2026.

4.2 United Kingdom — FCA, PRA, BoE, ICO

  • FCA / PRA / BoE Operational Resilience policy — fully embedded March 2025; identify important business services, set impact tolerances, test through severe-but-plausible scenarios.
  • UK Critical Third Parties (CTP) regime under FSMA 2023 — brings hyperscalers and other critical vendors under direct UK regulator oversight from 2024.
  • FCA Consumer Duty — July 2023; raises the bar on consumer outcomes, including fraud and scam prevention.
  • FCA APP fraud reimbursement — mandatory from October 2024; sender and receiver PSPs split the cost 50/50.
  • UK GDPR + ICO, NCSC Cyber Assessment Framework (CAF), NCSC Cyber Essentials.

4.3 India — RBI, SEBI, IRDAI, MeitY, DPDP

  • RBI Cyber Security Framework for Banks (2016, regularly updated) and the RBI Master Direction on IT Governance, Risk, Controls and Assurance Practices (Nov 2023).
  • RBI Master Direction on Outsourcing of IT Services (April 2023).
  • RBI Storage of Payment System Data (April 2018) — payments data localised in India.
  • RBI Digital Lending Guidelines (Sep 2022, amended 2024).
  • SEBI Cybersecurity and Cyber Resilience Framework for Market Infrastructure Institutions, 2024 update.
  • IRDAI Information and Cyber Security Guidelines for insurers.
  • DPDP Act 2023 — Digital Personal Data Protection Act; rules notified 2024–2025.
  • CERT-In Directions (April 2022) — incident reporting within 6 hours; log retention 180 days in India.

4.4 United States — federal and state

  • NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 — Feb 2024; the de-facto industry baseline; six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover.
  • NYDFS Part 500 — New York DFS Cybersecurity Regulation; 2023 amendment adds CISO certification and 72-hour incident reporting.
  • OCC Heightened Standards, Fed SR 23-4 third-party risk management, FDIC FFIEC IT Examination Handbook.
  • SEC Cybersecurity Disclosure Rule (July 2023) — 4-business-day disclosure of material incidents on Form 8-K.
  • GLBA Safeguards Rule (2003, 2021 update), Sarbanes-Oxley (SOX) for ICFR.
  • Executive Order 14028 (2021) — federal supply-chain rules; mandates SBOM for federal-vendor software; influences private sector.
  • NIST SP 800-53 / 800-171 / 800-207 (Zero Trust).

4.5 Singapore, Hong Kong, Australia, Brazil

  • MAS TRM Guidelines (Singapore, updated 2021); MAS Notice 644 / 658 on outsourcing and BCM; MAS Cyber Hygiene Notice; MAS FEAT principles for AI; Veritas consortium for AI governance.
  • HKMA C-RAF (Cyber Resilience Assessment Framework) + iCAST intelligence-led testing.
  • Australia APRA CPS 234 (2019) Information Security Prudential Standard; CPS 230 Operational Risk Management in force July 2025; Privacy Act reforms 2024.
  • Brazil LGPD + Banco Central Cyber Resilience Resolution 4893/2021.
  • UAE CBUAE Information and Cyber Security Standards; SAMA Cybersecurity Framework (Saudi Arabia); Israel Cyber Defense Methodology for Financial Institutions.

4.6 Cross-cutting — sanctions, AML, fraud

  • OFAC, EU sanctions, UK OFSI, India MEA — sanctions screening across SWIFT messages, card transactions, customer onboarding.
  • FATF Recommendations — global AML / CFT baseline; transposed into local law (PMLA in India, BSA in US, MLR in UK, AMLD6 in EU).
  • PCI DSS 4.0 — March 2025 fully in force; payment-card data security baseline.
  • SWIFT CSCF — Customer Security Controls Framework, annual attestation by every SWIFT user.

5. Advanced — zero trust, PQC, AI risk, fraud, resilience Advanced

5.1 Zero trust — beyond the slogan

Zero trust is not a product; it is an architectural stance with five practical components, derived from NIST SP 800-207:

  • Identity-centric access — every request authenticated against an IdP, every action authorised against policy.
  • Device posture — only known, healthy devices reach sensitive resources; integrated with MDM and EDR signals.
  • Microsegmentation — east-west traffic is policy-controlled, not flat.
  • Continuous evaluation — risk signals (geo, behaviour, time) re-evaluated mid-session; tokens short-lived.
  • Encrypted everywhere — TLS 1.3 / mTLS between every hop.

5.2 Cryptographic agility and PQC migration

Post-quantum migration is a 5–10 year programme. The playbook regulators are signalling:

  1. Cryptographic inventory — every place the bank uses public-key crypto: TLS, code signing, JWT, payments, document signing, archival storage.
  2. Crypto-agility — code that lets you swap algorithms without rewriting; abstract the primitives, version everything.
  3. Hybrid TLS — combine a classical and a PQC algorithm in TLS; if either is broken, traffic is still safe. Drafts implemented by AWS, Cloudflare, Google.
  4. Long-lived data — anything that must be confidential for >10 years (mortgages, insurance, trade tickets, archives) is at risk from ‘harvest now, decrypt later’ — migrate first.
  5. Vendor pressure — every HSM, KMS, PKI and SSL-termination vendor needs a PQC roadmap; the BFSI bar is ‘FIPS 203 / 204 / 205 by 2026’.

5.3 AI security and AI risk

  • OWASP LLM Top 10 (2023, updated 2024) — Prompt Injection, Insecure Output Handling, Training Data Poisoning, Model Denial of Service, Supply Chain Vulnerabilities, Sensitive Information Disclosure, Insecure Plugin Design, Excessive Agency, Overreliance, Model Theft.
  • NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF, 2023) and Generative AI Profile (2024).
  • EU AI Act high-risk categorisation for credit scoring and biometric ID; conformity assessment, post-market monitoring.
  • MAS FEAT principles — Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency for AI in finance.
  • Singapore Model AI Governance Framework v2 (2024) for generative AI.
  • Model registries and AI BoMs — track every model, dataset, prompt, and dependency; MLflow, AWS SageMaker Model Registry, Vertex AI.

5.4 Fraud and AML — the operational front line

  • Real-time transaction monitoring — Feedzai, NICE Actimize, SAS, Fiserv (Verafin), FICO Falcon, Featurespace (Visa), ThetaRay.
  • AML case management — NICE Actimize, Oracle FCCM, SAS AML, SymphonyAI Sensa-NetReveal, ComplyAdvantage.
  • Sanctions screening — Accuity (LSEG World-Check), Dow Jones Risk & Compliance, Refinitiv World-Check.
  • Authorised Push Payment fraud — UK PSR mandatory reimbursement Oct 2024; Confirmation of Payee, behavioural analytics, friction at the right moments.
  • Card fraud — 3-D Secure 2.x with EMV 3DS; risk-based authentication; tokenisation of card data.

5.5 Resilience patterns regulators expect to see

  • Important business services — named, with mapped dependencies and impact tolerances.
  • Severe-but-plausible scenarios — tested at least annually; documented; reviewed at board level.
  • Threat-led penetration testing (TLPT) — TIBER-EU under DORA; CBEST under UK; iCAST under HKMA. The bank does not know in advance which service the red team will hit.
  • Cyber recovery vault / immutable backups — isolated, immutable copy of critical data; tested restoration.
  • Exit plan from cloud / critical vendor — DORA, RBI, PRA all expect a documented, tested exit. Not a paper plan.

7. Worked examples — five real BFSI security stories Intermediate

Worked example — ICBC London — the November 2023 ransomware lessons
  1. ICBC Financial Services in New York was hit by Lockbit ransomware in November 2023, disrupting US Treasury settlements; trades had to be settled manually with USB drives carried between offices.
  2. Lessons drawn industry-wide: critical-third-party concentration risk is real; manual fallback procedures must be tested; backups alone do not give recovery if identity is also compromised.
  3. Regulator response: faster pace of TLPT under DORA, OCC and Fed letters on ransomware preparedness, FS-ISAC playbooks updated.
Worked example — Capital One 2019 — what really happened, and why it is still cited
  1. A former AWS engineer exploited a Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF) vulnerability in a misconfigured ModSecurity WAF rule to obtain temporary AWS credentials.
  2. Those credentials were attached to an over-permissioned IAM role that could read S3 buckets containing 100M+ customer records.
  3. Detection was delayed because the data egress looked like normal application behaviour and GuardDuty was not yet tuned to flag it.
  4. Resolution drove industry-wide adoption of: IMDSv2 (blocks SSRF-fetch of credentials), least-privilege IAM by default, automated config review, anomaly detection on egress.
  5. Why it still matters: the failure was at L5–L6 (application + identity), not in the cloud primitives; cloud is as secure as your configuration discipline.
Worked example — Standard Chartered passwordless workforce login
  1. Standard Chartered moved 90,000+ employees to FIDO2 passkeys / Windows Hello on Entra ID by 2024.
  2. Phishing-resistant MFA mandatory for all production access; CyberArk for privileged session recording.
  3. Reported >50% reduction in password-related helpdesk tickets and meaningful drop in credential-phishing incidents.
Worked example — Indian Account Aggregator — security in a consent-led ecosystem
  1. AAs and FIPs and FIUs all use mTLS with eKYC + ReBIT-defined consent artefacts.
  2. Consent artefacts are cryptographically signed by the customer; AAs never see plaintext financial data — the FIP encrypts to the FIU’s public key.
  3. RBI reviews AAs as Non-Banking Financial Companies (NBFC-AA); CERT-In incident reporting applies.
Worked example — EU bank preparing for DORA — first 12 months
  1. Month 1–3: regulatory gap analysis vs DORA RTS and ITS; ICT risk framework mapped to ISO 27001 / NIST CSF 2.0.
  2. Month 4–6: third-party register populated; ‘critical ICT third party’ designation reviewed with ECB; concentration risk quantified.
  3. Month 7–9: incident classification engine deployed in SIEM; major-incident notification playbooks rehearsed under EBA timelines.
  4. Month 10–12: TIBER-EU TLPT scoping with national competent authority; cyber recovery vault tested; board attestation prepared.

8. Questions a leader asks in any security review Basic

  1. Who are our identity providers — workforce, customer, machine — and is MFA phishing-resistant for all privileged access?
  2. What is our cryptographic inventory, and what is the PQC migration plan?
  3. Where are our HSMs, who can access them, and which data is HYOK / EKM vs cloud-managed?
  4. What is our zero-trust state — by service, not just as a slogan? Which services still rely on network-perimeter trust?
  5. How quickly can we rotate certificates and secrets estate-wide? When did we last test a mass rotation?
  6. What is our SBOM and supply-chain coverage; can we answer ‘are we exposed to library X?’ in under 24 hours?
  7. What did our last red-team / TLPT exercise find, and have those findings been closed?
  8. What is our incident-reporting clock under DORA / NYDFS / SEC / RBI / MAS, and have we rehearsed hitting them?
  9. How are AI / ML models inventoried; do high-risk models have documented evaluation and post-market monitoring?
  10. What is our APP fraud reimbursement exposure and control posture?
  11. Where is our cyber recovery vault; when did we last fully restore from it?
  12. What is the credible exit plan from each critical vendor / cloud, and when did we test it?

9. Red flags + topic glossary Basic

Red flag — push back if you hear this
  • ‘We have ISO 27001, we’re secure.’ — ISO 27001 is a management-system standard; it tells you the bank has processes, not that they work. Test outcomes, not certificates.
  • ‘Zero trust means we replaced the VPN.’ — Zero trust is identity, device, segmentation, continuous evaluation and encryption. ZTNA is one corner.
  • ‘MFA is on, we’re fine.’ — SMS and TOTP MFA are phishable; modern attackers run real-time relay phishing kits. Phishing-resistant FIDO2 / passkeys are the bar.
  • ‘PQC is years away.’ — Long-lived data is ‘harvest-now, decrypt-later’. Inventory and migration must start now.
  • ‘The cloud is secure by default.’ — The shared-responsibility model says you own configuration. Capital One, Pegasus Airlines, Toyota, MOVEit — all configuration failures.
  • ‘We outsource the SOC, so we’re covered.’ — Regulators hold you accountable; outsourcing is a delivery choice, not a transfer of liability.
  • ‘DORA only matters if we’re EU-based.’ — Any bank operating in the EU is in scope; many UK and Asian groups are aligning globally to avoid running two regimes.
  • ‘Backups are immutable, ransomware can’t hurt us.’ — Only if your identity domain is also isolated from the backup domain; otherwise the attacker who took your AD can take your backups.
  • ‘Our AI is behind the firewall, no governance needed.’ — Not under EU AI Act, MAS FEAT, or NIST AI RMF. Risk-classify and document.
  • ‘We rotate keys yearly, we’re compliant.’ — Rotation cadence depends on classification and on exposure events; fixed annual rotation is yesterday’s baseline.

Glossary for this topic

  • IAM / CIAM — Identity and Access Management / Customer IAM — workforce / customer identity stacks.
  • OIDC / SAML / OAuth 2.0 — Identity and authorisation protocols — OpenID Connect / SAML 2.0 / OAuth 2.0.
  • FIDO2 / WebAuthn / passkey — Public-key, phishing-resistant authentication standards.
  • MFA / SCA / RBA — Multi-Factor / Strong Customer / Risk-Based Authentication.
  • PAM — Privileged Access Management — CyberArk, BeyondTrust, Delinea, HashiCorp Boundary.
  • RBAC / ABAC / ReBAC — Authorisation models — role / attribute / relationship based.
  • OPA / Cedar / Kyverno — Policy-as-code engines.
  • HSM — Hardware Security Module — tamper-resistant key custody (Thales, Entrust, Utimaco, IBM, AWS / Azure / GCP / OCI Cloud HSMs).
  • FIPS 140-3 — US/Canadian standard for cryptographic modules; Level 3 typical for BFSI HSMs.
  • KMS / BYOK / HYOK / EKM — Key Management Service / Bring-Your-Own-Key / Hold-Your-Own-Key / External Key Manager.
  • PKI — Public Key Infrastructure — internal CAs, external CAs, eIDAS QTSPs.
  • PQC — Post-Quantum Cryptography — NIST FIPS 203 / 204 / 205, finalised Aug 2024.
  • Crypto-agility — Code that can swap cryptographic primitives without rewriting; precondition for PQC migration.
  • Zero Trust / ZTNA / SASE — ‘Never trust, always verify’ architecture; ZTNA replaces VPN; SASE fuses ZTNA + SD-WAN + CASB + SWG + FWaaS.
  • EDR / XDR / SSPM / CNAPP / CSPM — Endpoint / extended detection-response; SaaS Security Posture; Cloud-Native Application Protection; Cloud Security Posture Management.
  • SAST / DAST / SCA / SBOM — Static / Dynamic application security testing; Software Composition Analysis; Software Bill of Materials.
  • SIEM / SOAR — Security Information & Event Management / Security Orchestration, Automation and Response.
  • MITRE ATT&CK — Knowledge base of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures.
  • TLPT / TIBER-EU / CBEST / iCAST — Threat-Led Penetration Testing frameworks (EU, UK, HK).
  • DORA — EU Digital Operational Resilience Act — in force 17 January 2025.
  • NIS2 — EU Network and Information Security Directive 2 — applies from October 2024.
  • EU AI Act — EU regulation on AI — in force August 2024, phased through August 2026.
  • EU CRA — Cyber Resilience Act — adopted 2024; product-side cyber rules including SBOM.
  • UK CTP regime — UK Critical Third Parties under FSMA 2023.
  • FCA Consumer Duty / Operational Resilience — UK consumer-outcome and resilience policy regimes.
  • RBI IT Governance MD / Cyber Security Framework — India central-bank IT and cyber rules; 2023 update.
  • DPDP Act 2023 — India Digital Personal Data Protection Act.
  • NYDFS Part 500 — New York DFS Cybersecurity Regulation; 2023 amendments.
  • NIST CSF 2.0 — Cybersecurity Framework 2.0 — Feb 2024; six functions Govern/Identify/Protect/Detect/Respond/Recover.
  • MAS TRM / FEAT — Singapore Technology Risk Management Guidelines / Fairness, Ethics, Accountability, Transparency principles for AI.
  • HKMA C-RAF — Cyber Resilience Assessment Framework, Hong Kong.
  • APRA CPS 234 / CPS 230 — Australian prudential information-security and operational-risk standards.
  • FATF / AML / CFT — Financial Action Task Force; Anti-Money Laundering / Counter-Financing of Terrorism.
  • PCI DSS 4.0 — Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard; March 2025 fully in force.
  • SWIFT CSCF — Customer Security Controls Framework — annual attestation by every SWIFT user.
